#### Dates

The course is taken in conjunction with your Master's thesis. The schedule is

### Description

This reading course is taken alongside your Master's thesis and its content
should be directly linked to the topic of your thesis. The aim is to give you
the time and structure to read deeply in the area surrounding your thesis,
beyond what you would typically cover while focused on the research itself.

Working with your thesis supervisor, you will assemble a small reading list —
typically three to five items — consisting of foundational papers, influential
follow-up works, and/or one or two chapters from a canonical textbook in the
area. Together these should map out the intellectual context in which your
thesis sits: where the ideas came from, how they developed, and where the
current research frontier lies.

### AI policy

### Assessment

There is no examination. You will be assessed on the following piece of
coursework, which should be developed in dialogue with your thesis supervisor.

1. **Written report** of approximately twenty A4 pages (for 6 ECTS - scaled
   proportionately for other ECTS values), prepared in LaTeX, covering the
   items on your reading list. The report can be structured around the five
   roles detailed below, applied across the reading list as a whole rather than
   one role per paper. The report should be substantially different to the
   literature review submitted in the Masters thesis.

Grading is based around the existing Masters thesis grading scheme, excluding
notions of research quality.

### Schedule

The written report is due on the same day as the written Masters thesis
submission.

### Outline

You are welcome to use the 'roles' approach used in the Selected Topics in
MMCS course from Semester 3.

The following roles provide suggestions for the content and structure of the
report. Unlike in a group seminar, you are responsible for all five roles;
together they give you a balanced view of the literature surrounding your
thesis.

* [The Historians]({{ site.baseurl }}{% link roles/historians.md %}). Understand
  the historical context of the works on your reading list; prior works, the
  authors' biographies, the general technological/political/cultural context
  of the time. How did the ideas underpinning your thesis come to be?
* [The Technicians]({{ site.baseurl }}{% link roles/technicians.md %}). Read
  the works with care and produce a clean technical summary of the main
  results, in your own notation.
* [The Experimentalists]({{ site.baseurl }}{% link roles/experimentalists.md %}).
  Where appropriate, reproduce key results in a simple dynamic programming
  language, e.g. Python or MATLAB. 
* [The Futurists]({{ site.baseurl }}{% link roles/futurists.md %}). Understand
  the impact of the works in subsequent academic research and in industry.
  Trace how the ideas have evolved up to the current research frontier — and
  in particular, up to the question your thesis addresses.
* [Critics]({{ site.baseurl }}{% link roles/critics.md %}). Critique the works
  constructively. What was clear? What was not? Are there omissions? Are the
  arguments strong?
